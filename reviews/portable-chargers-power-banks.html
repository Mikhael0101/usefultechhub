Understanding Power Bank Specs

Capacity: mAh vs Wh

mAh (milliamp-hours) is the marketing number, but Wh (watt-hours) is what actually matters. A 10,000mAh bank at 3.7V = 37Wh. Due to conversion losses, expect about 60-70% usable capacity.

  • 5,000mAh: ~1 full phone charge
  • 10,000mAh: ~2-3 phone charges
  • 20,000mAh: ~4-6 phone charges or 1 laptop charge
  • 26,800mAh: Maximum TSA-allowed for flights (100Wh)

Charging Speed: Watts Matter

  • 10-12W: Standard charging, fine for overnight
  • 18-20W: Fast charging for most phones
  • 30W: Fast charging for phones + slower laptop charging
  • 45-65W: Full-speed laptop charging
  • 100W+: Fast charging for MacBook Pro 16" and similar
TSA Rules: Power banks must be in carry-on luggage, not checked bags. Maximum 100Wh (about 27,000mAh) without airline approval. Larger banks (100-160Wh) require airline permission.

How to Choose

For Phone-Only Users

The Anker PowerCore Slim 10000 PD is the sweet spot. 10,000mAh gives you 2-3 charges, 20W is fast enough for any phone, and the slim design is pocket-friendly. Don't overthink it.

For Laptop Users

Check your laptop's charging requirements. Most ultrabooks need 45-65W; MacBook Pro 16" wants 96-140W. The Anker 737 (140W) handles everything, while the Baseus Blade (100W) covers most laptops at a lower price.

For Travelers

Stay under 100Wh (27,000mAh) to avoid TSA hassles. The Nitecore NB10000 is ultralight for backpackers. For longer trips, the Anker 737 at 86.4Wh maximizes capacity while staying flight-legal.

Pro tip: Buy from reputable brands (Anker, Nitecore, Baseus, RAVPower). Cheap no-name power banks often lie about capacity and can be fire hazards. The savings aren't worth it.
