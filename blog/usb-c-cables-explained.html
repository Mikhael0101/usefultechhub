USB-C Cables Explained: Not All Cables Are Equal

January 3, 2026 · 6 min read · By Tech Team

USB-C is everywhere—phones, laptops, tablets, and accessories. But here's the problem: not all USB-C cables are the same. Using the wrong cable can mean slow charging, failed data transfers, or no video output. Let's clear up the confusion.

The USB-C Connector vs. USB Standards

This is where confusion starts. USB-C is just the physical connector shape—the oval, reversible plug. But what the cable actually does depends on the USB standard it supports.

Think of it like this: all USB-C cables have the same plug, but they have different "capabilities" inside.

USB Standards Explained

Standard Speed Common Use
USB 2.0 480 Mbps Charging, basic data
USB 3.0 / 3.1 Gen 1 5 Gbps Fast data transfer
USB 3.1 Gen 2 10 Gbps External SSDs
USB 3.2 Gen 2x2 20 Gbps High-speed storage
USB4 40 Gbps Everything
Thunderbolt 3/4 40 Gbps Pro peripherals, displays

Why Cheap Cables Are Often USB 2.0

That $3 USB-C cable from the gas station? It's probably USB 2.0. It'll charge your phone slowly and transfer data at a crawl. There's no law requiring manufacturers to label cable speeds, so cheap cables default to the minimum.

Warning: A USB 2.0 cable transferring from an external SSD is 10x slower than a USB 3.0 cable. A 10GB transfer takes 3 minutes vs. 30 minutes.

Power Delivery (PD): Charging Speed

USB Power Delivery is separate from data speed. It determines how fast your device charges.

  • Standard USB: 5V, 2.4A (12W) – Slow phone charging
  • USB PD 3.0: Up to 100W – Fast laptop charging
  • USB PD 3.1: Up to 240W – Gaming laptops

Your cable must support the wattage you need. A cheap cable might only handle 15W even if your charger outputs 100W.

Video Output: DisplayPort Alt Mode

Some USB-C cables can carry video signals to monitors. This requires DisplayPort Alt Mode support. Not all cables have this—typically only USB 3.1+ or Thunderbolt cables.

Tip: If your USB-C cable won't output video to a monitor, try a different cable. Many USB-C cables don't support video.

How to Identify Good Cables

  1. Check the packaging – Look for "USB 3.2", "10Gbps", or "100W PD"
  2. Look for logos – USB-IF certified cables have official logos
  3. Feel the cable – Thicker cables usually have more wires inside
  4. Buy from reputable brands – Anker, Belkin, Apple, Cable Matters
