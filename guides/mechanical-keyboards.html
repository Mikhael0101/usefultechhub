Mechanical Keyboards Buying Guide: Everything You Need to Know

January 3, 2026 · 12 min read · By Tech Team

Mechanical keyboards offer a superior typing experience, but choosing one can be overwhelming. This guide breaks down everything you need to know—switch types, keyboard sizes, features, and what actually matters for your use case.

Why Mechanical Keyboards?

Unlike membrane keyboards, mechanical keyboards use individual switches under each key. Benefits include:

  • Better typing feel – Tactile feedback and consistent actuation
  • Durability – Rated for 50-100 million keystrokes vs 5-10 million for membrane
  • Repairability – Individual switches can be replaced
  • Customization – Swap keycaps, switches, and more
  • Faster response – Better for gaming and fast typists

Switch Types Explained

The switch is the most important component. There are three main categories:

Linear Switches

Smooth keystroke with no bump or click. Preferred by gamers for fast, consistent keypresses.

  • Cherry MX Red – Light, 45g actuation force
  • Cherry MX Black – Heavier, 60g actuation force
  • Gateron Yellow – Budget-friendly, smooth feel

Tactile Switches

Noticeable bump when the key actuates. Great for typing—you feel when the keypress registers.

  • Cherry MX Brown – Light tactile bump, quiet
  • Cherry MX Clear – Stronger tactile bump
  • Gateron Brown – Smoother than Cherry Browns

Clicky Switches

Tactile bump plus an audible click sound. Satisfying but loud—not office-friendly.

  • Cherry MX Blue – Classic clicky switch
  • Cherry MX Green – Heavier clicky switch
  • Kailh Box White – Sharper click
Switch Type Feel Sound Best For
Linear Smooth Quiet Gaming
Tactile Bumpy Moderate Typing
Clicky Bumpy + Click Loud Typing (home use)
Pro tip: If you're unsure, start with tactile switches (Browns). They're versatile for both typing and gaming.

Keyboard Sizes

Keyboards come in various sizes. Smaller = more desk space but fewer dedicated keys.

Full Size (100%)

~104 keys. Includes numpad. Best if you use number pad frequently.

Tenkeyless (TKL/80%)

~87 keys. No numpad. Most popular size—good balance.

75%

~84 keys. Compact TKL. Function row + arrows in tight layout.

65%

~68 keys. No function row. Keeps arrows and some nav keys.

60%

~61 keys. Ultra compact. No arrows, no function row. Uses layers.

Hot-Swappable vs Soldered

Hot-swappable keyboards let you change switches without soldering. This is huge for beginners—you can experiment with different switches easily.

Soldered keyboards have switches permanently attached. Cheaper but no switch customization without desoldering.

Recommendation: If it's your first mechanical keyboard, strongly consider hot-swappable. The flexibility is worth the small price premium.

Features That Matter

Build Quality

  • Aluminum case – Premium feel, heavier, better acoustics
  • Plastic case – Lighter, cheaper, still functional
  • Gasket mount – Switches mounted on gaskets for softer typing feel

Connectivity

  • Wired USB – No latency, no battery concerns
  • Wireless 2.4GHz – Low latency wireless via dongle
  • Bluetooth – Convenient but higher latency
  • Triple-mode – All three options (best flexibility)

Keycaps

  • ABS plastic – Smooth, develops shine over time
  • PBT plastic – Textured, more durable, won't get shiny
  • Double-shot legends – Legends molded in, never fade

Software

Good software lets you remap keys, create macros, and customize RGB. QMK/VIA support is a plus for enthusiasts.

Common Mistakes to Avoid

  • Buying based on looks alone – Test switches if possible or buy hot-swap
  • Ignoring stabilizers – Bad stabs make spacebar and larger keys feel mushy
  • Overspending first time – Start mid-range, learn what you like
  • Getting clicky for office – Your coworkers will hate you

Bottom Line

For most people, a TKL or 75% hot-swappable keyboard with tactile switches is the sweet spot. It gives you flexibility to experiment while being practical for everyday use. Spend $60-100 for your first board—you'll get a great experience without going overboard.
