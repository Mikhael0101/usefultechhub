Gaming Peripherals Buying Guide: Mice, Keyboards & Headsets

Your peripherals directly impact gaming performance. A bad mouse or keyboard can hold you back, while the right gear enhances your gameplay. This guide covers what actually matters for competitive and casual gaming.

Part 1: Gaming Mice

Sensor

The sensor is the mouse's eye. Modern optical sensors from PixArt and Logitech Hero are all excellent. Key specs:

  • DPI: Sensitivity. 800-1600 is common for gaming. Ignore marketing hype about 25,000+ DPI—nobody uses it.
  • Polling rate: How often mouse reports position. 1000Hz is standard. 4000Hz+ is overkill for most.
  • Tracking speed: Maximum speed sensor can track. 400+ IPS is plenty.
Truth: Any modern gaming mouse ($30+) has a sensor good enough for pro play. Focus on shape, weight, and build quality instead.

Shape & Grip Style

This is the most important factor. Mice suit different grip styles:

Palm Grip

Entire hand rests on mouse. Need larger ergonomic shapes.

Claw Grip

Palm touches back, fingers arched. Medium mice, often ambidextrous.

Fingertip Grip

Only fingertips touch. Small, lightweight mice work best.

Weight

Lighter mice are easier to move quickly but provide less stability. Preference varies:

  • Ultralight (<60g): FPS games, fast flicks, arm aimers
  • Light (60-80g): Sweet spot for most gamers
  • Medium (80-100g): Balanced, good for MOBAs/strategy
  • Heavy (100g+): More control, older preference

Wired vs Wireless

Modern wireless (Logitech Lightspeed, Razer HyperSpeed) has zero perceptible latency. Considerations:

  • Wired: No battery, lighter, cheaper, cable drag
  • Wireless: Freedom, needs charging, heavier, costs more

Part 2: Gaming Keyboards

Switches for Gaming

Switch preference is personal, but here's the gaming perspective:

  • Linear (Red): Most popular for gaming. Smooth, fast, quiet.
  • Tactile (Brown): Good all-rounder. Slight bump helps typing.
  • Clicky (Blue): Satisfying but loud. Fine for gaming if you don't mind noise.

Speed vs Regular Switches

"Speed" switches have shorter actuation distance (~1.0mm vs 2.0mm). Marginally faster but more prone to accidental presses. Not necessary for most gamers.

Features That Matter for Gaming

  • N-key rollover: Register all simultaneous keypresses (essential)
  • Anti-ghosting: Prevent false inputs (essential)
  • 1000Hz polling: Fast response (standard now)
  • Hot-swap: Try different switches (nice to have)
  • PBT keycaps: Don't get shiny (nice to have)

Size for Gaming

Smaller keyboards give more mouse space—important for low-sensitivity players:

  • 60%: Maximum mouse room, learning curve
  • 65%: Keeps arrows, good balance
  • TKL: No numpad, popular choice
  • Full: If you need numpad for games

Part 3: Gaming Headsets

What Matters for Gaming Audio

  • Imaging: Ability to pinpoint sound direction (crucial for FPS)
  • Soundstage: How "wide" audio feels (helps awareness)
  • Comfort: You'll wear these for hours
  • Microphone quality: For team communication

Open-Back vs Closed-Back

Open-Back

Better soundstage and imaging. Sound leaks out/in. Best for quiet rooms.

Closed-Back

Noise isolation. More bass. Good if others are nearby or environment is noisy.

Wireless Latency

2.4GHz wireless headsets (via USB dongle) have near-zero gaming latency. Bluetooth has noticeable delay—avoid for competitive gaming.

Surround Sound

Virtual 7.1 surround is mostly marketing. Stereo headphones with good imaging are often better. Some games even perform worse with virtual surround.

Pro tip: Many pro gamers use stereo audiophile headphones + separate mic. It often sounds better than gaming headsets at the same price.

Bottom Line

For mice, shape and weight matter more than sensor specs. For keyboards, switches are personal—try before buying if possible. For headsets, comfort and imaging beat flashy features. Don't overspend chasing marginal gains—the skill gap between a $50 and $150 mouse is minimal. Invest in what feels right for you.
